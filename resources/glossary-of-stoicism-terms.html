Glossary of Stoicism Terms

The Stoic Fellowship has curated a list of common Stoicism terms and their definitions.

Apatheia
The state of being free from emotional disturbance and suffering; emphasizing tranquility.
Areté
Excellence or virtue in fulfilling one's purpose; the highest moral state achievable.
Asceticism
The practice of strict self-discipline and abstention from indulgence, typically for spiritual reasons.
Ataraxia
A state of serene calmness or imperturbability; the ultimate goal for Stoics.
Axiom
A self-evident truth that requires no proof, forming the basis of Stoic logic.
Categorical Imperative
Universal principles for ethical behavior, aligning with Stoic ethics' emphasis on universal principles.
Cosmopolitanism
The idea that all human beings are members of a single community based on shared morality.
Cynicism
A school of thought emphasizing living in virtue in agreement with nature.
Dichotomy of Control
Distinguishing between things that are within our control and those that are not.
Discourses
Text by Epictetus, laying out the principles of Stoic philosophy.
Duty (Kathekon)
Actions considered appropriate and expected for individuals, as part of living a virtuous life.
Eikasia
The lowest level of knowledge, representing illusion or mere guesswork about reality.
Empirical Wisdom
Knowledge gained through experience, emphasizing learning from the world to improve virtue.
Epictetus
A Greek Stoic philosopher known for his teachings on inner freedom and tranquility.
Eudaimonia
In Stoicism, eudaimonia refers to the highest human good, often translated as "happiness," "flourishing," or "living according to virtue." It is achieved through the rational pursuit of virtue, where an individual lives in harmony with nature and reason, embodying wisdom, courage, justice, and temperance in all aspects of life.
Euthymia
Euthymia in Stoicism denotes a state of inner tranquility and steadfastness, where an individual remains true to their own path and virtues, undistracted by external influences or the judgments of others. It encapsulates the pursuit of living in accordance with one’s own reason and nature, achieving serenity by focusing on what is within one’s control and accepting the natural flow of life.
Excellence (Virtue)
Virtue is the highest good in Stoicism, including wisdom, courage, justice, and temperance.
Fate (Heimarmene)
The Stoic belief in causal determinism, where everything happens according to a universal plan.
Freedom from Passion (Apathia)
Liberating oneself from the control of passions through reason and virtue.
Gnosis
Knowledge of spiritual mysteries; in Stoicism, knowledge of what is good, evil, and indifferent.
Harmony with Nature
Aligning one's life with the natural order and rational structure of the universe, leading to peace.
Hedonism
The philosophy that pleasure is the highest good; Stoicism proposes virtue as the most important goal.
Heraclitus
Pre-Socratic philosopher whose doctrines influenced Stoic metaphysics.
Hypolepsis
Assumption or acceptance; in Stoicism, the judgment or assent to an impression.
Indifferents (Adiaphora)
Things that are neither good nor bad in themselves and do not contribute to or detract from a happy life.
Internality
The focus on internal rather than external factors for achieving happiness.
Justice
One of the cardinal virtues in Stoicism, involving fairness, kindness, and respect for others.
Knowledge (Episteme)
True understanding or comprehension, as opposed to mere opinion.
Logos
The universal reason or the rational principle governing the cosmos.
Love of Fate (Amor Fati)
Embracing whatever life brings, seeing all experiences as necessary parts of the whole.
Materialism
The belief that the universe is composed of physical matter, including the mind.
Meditations
A series of personal writings by Marcus Aurelius, reflecting on Stoic philosophy.
Moderation (Sophrosyne)
The virtue of self-control and moderation, governing one's appetites and passions.
Moral Indifference
The belief that external events or material possessions do not inherently affect one's moral virtue.
Natural Law
Universal, rational laws governing nature and human conduct, which wise individuals strive to follow.
Nature, Living in Accordance
Understanding the nature of the universe and oneself, and aligning one's actions with this understanding.
Negative Visualization
Contemplation of potential losses or hardships to reduce their impact and to appreciate what one has.
Objectivity
Viewing and understanding the world as it truly is, without bias or the influence of personal feelings.
Obstacle as the Way
Viewing challenges and difficulties as opportunities for growth and the practice of virtue.
Opinion (Doxa)
Judgments or beliefs not based on certain knowledge or wisdom.
Passions
Irrational and harmful emotions that disturb the mind, such as fear, envy, and anger.
Pathos
Another term for the passions or emotions that are considered obstacles to wisdom and virtue.
Perception (Prosoche)
Continual awareness and attention to one's thoughts and actions, ensuring alignment with virtue.
Philosophy as a Way of Life
The belief that philosophy is not merely an academic discipline but a practical guide for living virtuously.
Phronesis
Practical wisdom or moral intelligence; the ability to navigate complex situations in a virtuous manner.
Physics
Study in Stoicism focusing on understanding the nature of the universe.
Pneuma
The breath of life, an active, generative principle connecting all living things to the universe.
Practical Wisdom (Phronesis)
The virtue allowing for the right decisions at the right time, especially in moral situations.
Preferable Indifferents
External things considered "preferably indifferent," meaning they are preferred but not necessary for a good life.
Rationality
The capacity for logical, reasoned thought, distinguishing humans from other animals.
Reason (Logos)
The capacity for logical thought, enabling humans to live in harmony with the universe's rational structure.
Resilience
The ability to endure pain or hardship, developed through understanding and accepting what we cannot control.
Sage
Someone who has attained moral and intellectual perfection in Stoicism.
Self-Control (Enkrateia)
The ability to exercise control over one's desires and impulses, essential for achieving virtue.
Self-Discipline
The ability to control one's emotions, desires, and actions in accordance with reason.
Self-Sufficiency (Autarkeia)
The sufficiency of virtue for a good life, independent of external circumstances.
Seneca
A Roman Stoic philosopher, statesman, and playwright who made significant contributions to Stoicism.
Socratic Dialogue
A method of teaching and inquiry through questions and answers to stimulate critical thinking and illuminate ideas.
Stoic Acceptance
Embracing life's events with equanimity, understanding everything happens according to the natural order.
Stoic Joy
Contentment and well-being from living a life of virtue, as opposed to fleeting pleasures.
Stoic Mindfulness (Prosoche)
Vigilance and awareness of one's thoughts, actions, and the present moment, aimed at virtuous and wise living.
Stoic Paradoxes
Statements that seem contradictory but are logical within Stoic philosophy.
Stoic Peace
The inner tranquility from mastering one's desires, fears, and emotions through reason.
Stoicism
A school of Hellenistic philosophy focusing on personal ethics informed by logic and views on the natural world.
Sympatheia
The interconnectedness and unity of all things in the universe.
Temperance (Sophrosyne)
The virtue of self-control and moderation, avoiding excess.
Theoretical Wisdom (Sophia)
Knowledge of the universe and human life, guiding one's life alongside practical wisdom.
Tranquility of Mind (Ataraxia)
A state of serene calmness, free from distress and worry, achieved through virtue and rationality.
Transience
The impermanent nature of all things, with an emphasis on memento mori to cultivate appreciation for the present.
Universal Nature
The rational, orderly system governing the cosmos, of which humans are a part.
Universal Reason (Logos)
The rational and orderly principle that governs the universe, inherent in human beings.
Value
In Stoicism, true value is found in virtue and rational living, not in external things.
Virtue Ethics
An ethical philosophy emphasizing the role of character and virtue rather than the consequences of actions.
Virtuous Circle
Practicing virtues leads to happiness, which in turn makes it easier to practice virtues, creating a positive feedback loop.
Volition
The power of using one’s will in accordance with reason and virtue.
Will
The faculty of choice or decision-making aligned with reason and virtue.
Wisdom
The knowledge of what is good and evil, including the discernment of what is in our control and what is not.
World Citizen
One's primary allegiance is to the universal community of human beings, rather than any local or national entities.
Xenia
Hospitality; underscores the Stoic value of cosmopolitanism and shared humanity.
Xenophobia
Fear or hatred of strangers or foreigners; Stoicism advocates against xenophobia through cosmopolitanism.
Zeno of Citium
The founder of Stoicism, emphasizing virtue as the highest good.
Zenonism
Referring to the teachings and philosophies of Zeno of Citium, particularly his emphasis on virtue.
