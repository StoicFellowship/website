Glossary of Stoicism Terms

The Stoic Fellowship has curated a list of common Stoicism terms and their definitions.

Apatheia
Apatheia in Stoicism denotes a state of tranquility and freedom from destructive emotions (pathos), achieved through living in accordance with reason and virtue. It signifies not the absence of emotions but the absence of uncontrolled, irrational passions that disturb the mind.
Areté
Excellence or virtue in fulfilling one's purpose; the highest moral state achievable.
Asceticism
In the context of Stoicism, asceticism refers to the practice of self-discipline and abstention from indulgence in physical pleasures, aiming to strengthen one's will and understanding of what is truly necessary for happiness. While not all Stoics adopt extreme forms of asceticism, they value moderation and control over desires to maintain freedom from external influences.
Assent
In Stoicism, assent refers to the conscious acceptance of impressions or thoughts by the rational mind, a key aspect of the Stoic theory of knowledge and action. It involves the critical evaluation of perceptions to determine their truth and alignment with reason before accepting them as the basis for judgment and action, emphasizing the role of personal judgment in shaping one's response to external events.
Ataraxia
Ataraxia is a state of serene calmness and untroubled mind, achieved through the absence of distress and emotional turmoil. In Stoicism, it results from understanding and accepting what is within one's control, aligning with nature, and practicing virtue.
Axiom
A self-evident truth that requires no proof, forming the basis of Stoic logic.
Cosmopolitanism
In Stoicism, cosmopolitanism is the idea that all humans belong to a single community, based on mutual respect and moral obligation, transcending local cultural or political boundaries. Stoics advocate for viewing oneself as a world citizen, emphasizing the interconnectedness and equality of all people in promoting justice and understanding.
Courage
In Stoicism, courage is not just physical bravery but also the moral courage to stand up for what is right, to face fears and difficulties with resilience, and to maintain integrity under pressure. It is one of the four cardinal virtues, essential for overcoming obstacles that impede virtuous living and for facing life's challenges with a calm and rational mindset.
Cynicism
A school of thought that emphasized living in agreement with nature. Those who followed the school were called Cynics and advocated for living in virtue in accordance with nature, often emphasizing asceticism and the rejection of conventional desires for wealth, power, and fame, principles that Stoics partially adopted and refined within their broader ethical system.
Dichotomy of Control
The dichotomy of control is a fundamental Stoic principle that distinguishes between things that are within our control (our own thoughts, actions, and responses) and those that are not (external events, the actions of others). Stoicism teaches that understanding and accepting this division is key to achieving tranquility, as it allows individuals to focus their efforts on their own virtues and responses, rather than on uncontrollable external circumstances.
Discourses
Text by Epictetus, laying out the principles of Stoic philosophy.
Duty (Kathekon)
Kathekon in Stoicism refers to actions that are in accordance with one's duties, derived from reason and the natural roles one occupies within society and humanity. These actions are considered appropriate and moral, reflecting a Stoic's commitment to live in harmony with nature and societal expectations.
Eikasia
Eikasia, though more prominently discussed within Plato's philosophy, pertains to the lowest level of understanding, characterized by the perception of images and shadows, rather than reality itself. In a Stoic context, while not explicitly named, the concept resonates with the idea of being misled by appearances and the importance of transcending superficial understandings to grasp the nature of things as they are.
Epictetus
Epictetus was a Greek Stoic philosopher of the 1st and 2nd centuries AD, who taught that philosophy is a way of life and not just a theoretical discipline. He emphasized the distinction between what is within our control and what is not, advocating for focusing on our own actions and attitudes to achieve a life of tranquility and virtue.
Eudaimonia
In Stoicism, eudaimonia refers to the highest human good, often translated as "happiness," "flourishing," or "living according to virtue." It is achieved through the rational pursuit of virtue, where an individual lives in harmony with nature and reason, embodying wisdom, courage, justice, and temperance in all aspects of life.
Euthymia
Euthymia in Stoicism denotes a state of inner tranquility and steadfastness, where an individual remains true to their own path and virtues, undistracted by external influences or the judgments of others. It encapsulates the pursuit of living in accordance with one’s own reason and nature, achieving serenity by focusing on what is within one’s control and accepting the natural flow of life.
Excellence (Virtue)
In Stoicism, virtue is considered the highest good and is defined as living in accordance with reason and nature. It encompasses wisdom, courage, justice, and temperance, and is the foundation for achieving eudaimonia, or a flourishing life.
Fate (Heimarmene)
In the context of Stoicism, Heimarmene refers to the concept of fate or destiny, understood as the rational order governing the cosmos and the interconnected web of causes and effects. Stoics believed that everything happens according to Heimarmene, encouraging acceptance of events as part of a logical plan, and focusing on what individuals can control — their perceptions, decisions, and actions.
Harmony with Nature
Harmony with nature in Stoicism refers to living according to the rational order of the universe, recognizing one's role within the greater whole and aligning one's actions with the natural laws and cycles. This principle underlines the importance of understanding the world and oneself, promoting a life of virtue, reason, and emotional resilience.
Hedonism
Hedonism, contrasting with Stoic principles, is the philosophy that pleasure or happiness is the highest good and ultimate aim of life. Stoicism, on the other hand, advocates for virtue as the highest good, viewing pleasure as indifferent and emphasizing self-control and moderation to achieve a tranquil and virtuous life.
Heraclitus
Heraclitus, a pre-Socratic philosopher, significantly influenced Stoicism with his doctrine of change and the unity of opposites, positing that the universe is in a constant state of flux and that opposites are necessary for life. Stoics adopted his view that life is like a river, ever-changing, and emphasized the importance of living in accordance with the natural order and embracing change as part of the cosmic plan.
Hypolepsis
Hypolepsis in Stoicism refers to the assent or agreement given to impressions or perceptions, a critical step in the process of forming judgments and opinions about external events. Stoics emphasize the importance of scrutinizing these impressions carefully to avoid false judgments and maintain tranquility and virtue.
Indifferents (Adiaphora)
Adiaphora in Stoicism refers to things that are morally indifferent, meaning they are neither inherently good nor bad, such as wealth, health, and reputation. Stoics believe that how one responds to and uses these indifferent things is what matters, aiming to maintain virtue and tranquility regardless of external circumstances.
Justice
In Stoicism, justice is one of the four cardinal virtues and it entails fairness, kindness, and the active promotion of harmony and the common good within society. It is based on the understanding that all humans are rational beings and thus deserve respect and consideration, guiding Stoics to act in ways that are beneficial to the community and the world at large.
Logic
In Stoicism, logic is not just the study of reasoning, but it encompasses the tools for discerning true from false, guiding ethical judgments and actions. It includes the examination of arguments, the nature of truth, and the processes of thinking and judgment, serving as the foundation for achieving clarity and consistency in one’s thoughts and aligning them with the rational order of the universe.
Logos
In Stoicism, Logos refers to the rational, organizing principle of the Universe, signifying reason, order, and divine intelligence permeating all things. It embodies the Stoic belief in a cosmos governed by rationality, where human reason is a microcosm of the cosmic reason, guiding individuals to live in accordance with nature and virtue.
Love of Fate (Amor Fati)
Embracing whatever life brings, seeing all experiences as necessary parts of the whole.
Marcus Aurelius
Marcus Aurelius, a Roman Emperor from 161 to 180 AD and a Stoic philosopher, is best known for his work "Meditations," a series of personal writings and reflections that offer insight into Stoic philosophy applied to governance and personal conduct. His thoughts emphasize the importance of rationality, self-discipline, and the pursuit of virtue amidst the trials of life and the duties of leadership. Marcus Aurelius remains a significant figure in Stoicism, embodying its principles in both his personal and public life.
Meditations
A series of personal writings by Marcus Aurelius, reflecting on Stoic philosophy.
Memento Mori
Memento mori, while not exclusively a Stoic term, is embraced within Stoicism as a reminder of the inevitability of death, encouraging mindfulness of the present moment and the impermanence of life. This reflection fosters a greater appreciation for life, motivates the pursuit of virtue, and helps maintain focus on what truly matters, free from trivial concerns and distractions.
Moderation (Sophrosyne)
Sophrosyne, a concept embraced by Stoicism and other ancient Greek philosophies, refers to an ideal of self-control, moderation, and balance, rooted in an understanding of one's true needs and desires. It is closely related to the Stoic virtue of temperance, advocating for a life lived in harmony with reason, where one exercises restraint and wisdom in all aspects of life.
Moral Indifference
Moral indifference in Stoicism refers to the perspective that external conditions, such as wealth, health, and social status, are neutral and do not contribute to one's moral virtue or happiness. Stoics believe that true good lies in virtuous living in accordance with reason, and thus, external circumstances are indifferent in terms of moral value.
Musonius Rufus
Musonius Rufus was a Roman Stoic philosopher of the 1st century CE, recognized as one of the four great Roman Stoics and the teacher of Epictetus. His teachings emphasize the importance of living a virtuous life in accordance with Stoic principles, advocating for equality between genders in education and the practical application of philosophy to daily life.
Natural Law
In Stoicism, natural law refers to the universal, rational principles that govern the cosmos, suggesting that human laws and ethics should align with these inherent principles of justice, virtue, and rational order. Stoics believe that by understanding and living in accordance with natural law, individuals can achieve a harmonious and virtuous life.
Negative Visualization
Negative visualization is a Stoic practice where one periodically imagines losing things they value or experiencing adverse events. This mental exercise is intended to cultivate gratitude for what one has and resilience against future misfortunes.
Opinion (Doxa)
In Stoicism, doxa refers to opinions or beliefs about things that are not directly within our control, often concerning what is considered good or bad. Stoics strive to distinguish between objective reality and doxa to maintain inner peace and virtue.
Pathos
In Stoicism, pathos refers to destructive emotions or passions that arise from false judgments about what is good or bad, leading one away from reason and virtue. Stoics aim to overcome pathos through rational assessment and alignment with nature, achieving a state of apatheia (freedom from such emotions).
Perception (Prosoche)
Prosoche in Stoicism is the practice of continuous, vigilant attention to one's thoughts, actions, and motivations, ensuring they align with the principles of virtue and rationality. It is a key aspect of Stoic discipline, enabling practitioners to live consciously and ethically in the present moment, mindful of their duties and the nature of their impressions.
Philosophy as a Way of Life
The belief that philosophy is not merely an academic discipline but a practical guide for living virtuously.
Phronesis
Phronesis, in Stoic philosophy, is the virtue of practical wisdom, referring to the ability to discern the appropriate course of action in any given situation based on reason and moral principles. It encompasses not just theoretical knowledge but also the intelligence and judgment necessary for living a virtuous and fulfilling life, guiding Stoics in making decisions that align with the natural order and their duties.
Physics
In Stoicism, physics refers to the study of the natural world and its governing laws, including the concepts of Logos (rational order) and pneuma (the vital breath connecting all things). Stoic physics is not merely scientific but is intertwined with ethics and logic, as understanding the nature of the universe is seen as essential for living in accordance with it, which in turn leads to a virtuous and fulfilling life.
Pneuma
Pneuma in Stoicism refers to the vital breath or spirit, a unifying principle that permeates the universe, linking all matter and imparting it with life and rationality. This concept is central to Stoic physics, illustrating how the cosmos is interconnected and governed by a rational order (Logos). Pneuma is seen as the binding force that ensures cohesion and stability in the natural world, embodying the Stoic belief in the unity of all things.
Preferable Indifferents
Preferable indifferents in Stoicism refer to things that, while not contributing to virtue—the sole good—are nonetheless chosen because they align with a rational and virtuous life, such as health, wealth, and education. These are considered "preferable" because they can facilitate a life in accordance with nature and virtue, but they are "indifferent" in terms of moral value and are not necessary for happiness or a good life.
Rationality
In Stoicism, rationality is the defining characteristic of human nature, distinguishing humans from other beings and enabling the practice of virtue. It involves the use of reason to navigate life, make ethical decisions, and understand the world, aligning one's actions with the universal order (Logos). Stoics see rationality as the pathway to living in harmony with nature and achieving a state of inner tranquility and moral integrity.
Reason (Logos)
In Stoicism, reason is the faculty that allows humans to discern the true nature of reality, guiding them to live in accordance with the rational structure of the universe, or Logos. It is through reason that individuals can practice virtue, make moral judgments, and achieve tranquility by distinguishing between what is within their control and what is not.
Sage
In Stoicism, a sage is considered the ideal wise person who has attained perfect virtue and wisdom, living in complete harmony with reason and thus in accordance with nature. This sage, though theoretically attainable, is a rare or even hypothetical figure, embodying the Stoic ideals of rationality, self-control, and emotional equanimity, free from the sway of destructive emotions.
Self-Control (Enkrateia)
Enkrateia, closely related to the concept of self-control and discipline, is valued in Stoicism for its emphasis on mastery over one's desires and impulses, ensuring that actions are governed by reason and virtue. It is essential for maintaining inner harmony and freedom, allowing one to live in accordance with rational principles and in alignment with the natural order of the universe.
Self-Sufficiency (Autarkeia)
Autarkeia in Stoicism denotes self-sufficiency, referring to a state where one's happiness and contentment are derived from within, independent of external circumstances or possessions. It underscores the Stoic belief that true good lies in one's own character and virtue, rather than in external achievements or material wealth, aiming for a life free from the influence of external forces on one's peace of mind.
Seneca
Seneca the Younger, a Roman Stoic philosopher, statesman, and playwright, made significant contributions to Stoicism, emphasizing practical ethics and the importance of personal virtue, rationality, and self-control in overcoming emotional turmoil. His works, including letters and essays, provide insight into Stoic philosophy applied to daily life, advocating for a life of simplicity, ethical integrity, and harmony with nature.
Socratic Dialogue
Socratic dialogue, while not a Stoic invention, significantly influenced Stoic methodology, involving a form of cooperative argumentative dialogue between individuals, based on asking and answering questions to stimulate critical thinking and illuminate ideas. Stoics, following Socrates' example, used dialogue as a means to explore ethical and philosophical problems, emphasizing the importance of logic and reason in uncovering truths about virtue, the good life, and the nature of the self.
Stoic Acceptance
Stoic acceptance involves embracing events and circumstances outside one’s control with tranquility and without distress, guided by the understanding that only one’s own virtues and actions are true sources of happiness. This principle is central to Stoic ethics, encouraging a focus on personal virtue and rational action in response to the inevitable challenges of life, thereby maintaining inner peace.
Stoic Joy
Stoic joy arises from living in accordance with virtue and reason, rather than from external circumstances or fleeting pleasures. It is a profound sense of contentment and well-being that comes from knowing one is living rightly, aligning with the Stoic belief that true happiness is found in ethical living and self-mastery.
Stoic Paradoxes
Stoic paradoxes are statements that seem contradictory to common beliefs but reflect deeper truths within Stoic philosophy. Examples include the idea that only the wise are truly rich or that virtue is the only good. These paradoxes challenge conventional values, emphasizing that true happiness and wealth come from virtue and wisdom, not material possessions or social status.
Stoic Peace
Stoic peace is a state of inner tranquility and freedom from disturbance, achieved by living in accordance with reason and virtue, and by accepting what is outside one’s control. This peace is cultivated through practices like reflection, mindfulness, and the disciplined control of one's reactions to external events, embodying the Stoic pursuit of harmony with the natural order of the universe.
Stoicism
Stoicism is an ancient Greek philosophy founded in the early 3rd century BC by Zeno of Citium, focusing on virtue, reason, and natural law as the paths to true happiness. It teaches the development of self-control and fortitude as a means of overcoming destructive emotions and living in harmony with the natural world, advocating for a life guided by wisdom, courage, justice, and temperance.
Sympatheia
Sympatheia in Stoicism refers to the interconnectedness and mutual interdependence of all things in the universe, emphasizing the idea that everything is part of a larger whole governed by the Logos. This concept encourages Stoics to view themselves not as isolated individuals but as integral parts of the cosmic order, fostering a sense of unity with others and a responsibility towards the common good.
Temperance (Sophrosyne)
Sophrosyne in the context of ancient Greek philosophy, including Stoicism, embodies the ideal of self-control, moderation, and balance, achieved through self-knowledge and wisdom. It is closely aligned with the Stoic virtue of temperance, guiding individuals to live in accordance with reason and to exercise restraint in their desires and actions, thereby contributing to their overall well-being and virtuous life.
Theoretical Wisdom (Sophia)
Sophia in Stoicism represents wisdom, one of the four cardinal virtues, encompassing both theoretical knowledge of the cosmos (philosophical wisdom) and practical wisdom (phronesis) in living according to nature. It involves the understanding and application of virtue, guiding Stoics in their decisions and actions towards a life of rationality, moral integrity, and harmony with the universal order.
Transience
In Stoicism, transience reflects the understanding that all external conditions and material things are impermanent and subject to change. This recognition encourages Stoics to focus on what is within their control—virtuous living and internal states—rather than external possessions or statuses, fostering resilience and peace in the face of life's inevitable changes.
Virtue
In Stoicism, virtue is considered the highest good and is defined as living in accordance with reason and nature. It encompasses wisdom, courage, justice, and temperance, and is the foundation for achieving eudaimonia, or a flourishing life.
Virtue Ethics
Virtue ethics, a cornerstone of Stoic philosophy, emphasizes the role of character and virtue in moral philosophy rather than the formulation of ethical rules or the consequences of actions. It posits that a good life is achieved through the cultivation of virtues such as wisdom, courage, justice, and temperance, guiding individuals toward making ethical choices that align with their best selves.
Volition
In Stoicism, volition refers to the faculty of will or choice, emphasizing the importance of intentional action aligned with virtue and reason. It highlights the Stoic belief in the power of the individual to control their responses to external events, even when those events themselves are beyond control, thereby maintaining inner tranquility and ethical integrity.
Wisdom
In Stoicism, wisdom is one of the four cardinal virtues, signifying the deep understanding and practical application of ethical principles, rational judgment, and the knowledge of what is truly good or bad. It encompasses both the theoretical grasp of the universe (philosophical wisdom) and the practical ability to navigate life's challenges (phronesis), guiding Stoics towards virtuous living and inner peace.
Xenia
Xenia, while not a term specifically from Stoicism but rather from ancient Greek culture, refers to the concept of hospitality and the generous and courteous treatment of guests or strangers. In a Stoic context, this practice can be related to the virtues of justice and kindness, reflecting the Stoic emphasis on universal brotherhood and the ethical treatment of others regardless of their status.
Zeno of Citium
Zeno of Citium was the founder of Stoicism, a philosophical school established in Athens around 300 BC. His teachings emphasized the importance of virtue as the highest good and advocated for living in accordance with nature and reason, aiming to achieve a life of tranquility and freedom from distress. Zeno's philosophy laid the groundwork for Stoic thought, focusing on ethics, logic, and the natural world's understanding.
